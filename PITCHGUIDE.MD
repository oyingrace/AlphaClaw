Part 1 - Pitch Clarity: Start with the tweet 

The formula - “We are [familiar thing] for [specific context]  e.g we are the stripe for Bitcoin- native payments on stacks or “we are the Uber for the commercial food industry “ 

The clarity test 
Say it to someone outside crypto. Do they get it in one shot? 
Make it sticky - something a judge can repeat to the next person in the room. 


Part 2: Problem Statement: Make them Feel It
Don’t describe a category of pain - name a real person in a real situation. Specific beats generic every time 

Weak - “Defi is hard to use “ Too vague. No human. No stakes. Judges have heard this a hundred times 

Strong - “A small business owner in Lagos can’t access dollar denominated savings without a US bank account” Real person. Real problem. Real stakes 

Back it up with a stat, a trend or a real observation. The test: Does someone nod their head when they hear it? 

Parts 3 & 4 
Solution and Value Proposition 

Your “aha” Moment 
Lead with the insight - what made you build this? Use plain language only. No jargon 
Use the Frame. “For [who], our solution helps them [do what] by [how].” Example: “For indie musicians, our platform helps them get paid instantly for streams using sBTC micropayments- no label, no middleman “ 

Why You, Why Now? 
Use the differentiation formula: 
“Unlike [existing solution], we [key differentiator] - which means [specific benefit].” 
What is the insight no one else has acted on? 
Who adopts this first ? 
Could this become a product, protocol or public good ?

Judging Criterion 
Stacks Alignment: Connect to the Bigger Mission 
This is scored directly. Be explicit - don’t assume judges will infer it 

1. Bitcoin Utility - How does your project make Bitcoin more useful in the real world? Be specific about the mechanism 
2. Stacks Adoption - How does your build grow the ecosystem? Show impact beyond the hackathon 
3. What you used - Clarity, sBTC, Proof of Transfer (PoX), stacks.js. Bitflow. USDCx 
“We choose Stacks because [specific reason] - and without it, this won’t be possible”. 

What kills a good pitch 
These are the most common mistakes - and every single one is avoidable 
1. Leading with the Tech - Judges need the problem first. Always. The tech is the proof, not the story. 
2. Jargon Overload - If you wouldn’t say it to a non-crypto friend , cut it. “Trustless cross-chain interoperability layer” loses everyone
3. No specific User - “Everyone” is not a user. Name someone real. Make judges picture a human being 
4. Skipping Stacks Alignment - Be intentional about your stack choice. It’s a second criterion - don’t leave it implied. 
5. Features instead of a story - Features inform. Stories persuade. A list of capabilities is not a pitch
